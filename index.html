New Zealand's longest navigable waterway

About the locations

Whanganui national park is located in New Zealand’s North island. The Whanganui National Park was established in 1987 to protect the forest and land. The Whanganui river is New Zealand's longest navigable waterway that is 290km-long and extends to the Tasman Sea. Along the the heart of Whanganui national park, experience canoeing and kayaking on the river whilist looking at the great view of the mountains and take a chance to jet boat to access the Bridge To Nowhere that were constructed in 1919 to provide farms for soldiers returning from World War I. Amoung the river, the national park has a vast landscape surrounding it with steep hills and mountains. A perfect spot to hike and explore New Zealand’s abundant native wildlife. Vist huts and pā (Village) to learn about Māori customs and traditions as well as listen to traditional stories.

Culture and community

The people of the Whanganui River are collectively called Ngāti Hau. Whanganui contains rich Māori history that have drawn tourists from around the world. Early Māori used the Whanganui River to trade and communicate among themselves. The river is their primary source of travel, and a source of physical and spiritual sustenance. Learn about traditional stories on the formation of the river and mountains. As well as life of the early Māori settlers, ancestoral groups and the influence of the European settlers.
